The Review

Hazelight's whole thing is asymmetric co-op built entirely around two players, and Split Fiction pushes that formula even further than It Takes Two by constantly swapping genres — one section is a platformer, the next is a twin-stick shooter, the next is something else entirely.

Never Long Enough To Get Old

Because each mechanic only sticks around for a level or two before the game moves to something new, nothing overstays its welcome. It's exhausting to describe and genuinely fun to actually play through with a second person.

Built Entirely For Two Players

As with prior Hazelight games, this isn't playable solo, and both players need their own copy or one person needs the (included) friend-pass style free trial access — check EA's current terms before assuming it's fully free for the second player.

How It Shakes Out

If you've got a regular co-op partner, this is an easy recommendation — inventive, funny, and never boring across its whole runtime. Just don't expect to touch it solo.

Pros
  • Constantly shifting mechanics keep every section fresh
  • Genuinely funny writing and character chemistry
  • Visually inventive across wildly different genre segments
  • Friend-pass style access lowers the cost barrier for the second player
Cons
  • Entirely unplayable solo by design
  • Some mechanics are more polished than others
  • Pacing can feel frantic if you prefer slower games
  • Story stakes are fairly thin compared to It Takes Two
