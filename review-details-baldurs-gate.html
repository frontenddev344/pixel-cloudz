The Review

Licensed D&D games have a rough track record, so we went into this one with modest expectations. Those expectations were wrong. Larian took what worked in Divinity: Original Sin 2 — let the player try anything, then actually account for it — and pointed a much bigger budget at it. The result is an RPG where it feels like the writers anticipated whatever weird idea you had.

The Reactivity Is Not Marketing Speak

"Your choices matter" shows up on the back of every RPG box these days, so we're usually skeptical. Here it's actually true. Companions bring up things you did three acts ago. Quests can be resolved by fighting, talking your way out, or doing something so ridiculous it clearly wasn't the "intended" solution but works anyway. It does cost the game some polish — Act 3 in Baldur's Gate itself is noticeably busier and rougher around the edges than the first two acts — but there's just so much authored content here that the trade-off feels worth it.

Combat Rewards Actually Using The Environment

Combat runs on 5th Edition D&D rules turned turn-based, and it wants you to use the battlefield — shove someone off a ledge, light an oil slick on fire, drop a chandelier on somebody's head. All of it works, and all of it feels earned rather than scripted. The full 5E class list is here too, subclasses and multiclassing included, so build variety isn't really a concern. If you want the game to punish you for a bad decision, Tactician and Honour modes will happily do that; if you just want the story, Explorer mode has your back.

The Cast Carries A Lot Of Weight

Shadowheart, Astarion, Gale, Karlach, Wyll, Lae'zel — the six core companions each get a fully voiced personal arc that can end in very different places depending on how you've treated them along the way. Almost every line of dialogue in the game is voiced, including characters you'll talk to once and never see again, which is the kind of production effort that's easy to take for granted until you notice how rare it actually is.

Performance Is Good, Mostly In Acts 1 and 2

PC performance holds up well outside of Act 3's dense urban sprawl, where even solid hardware can chug a bit. PS5 defaults to a stable 30fps mode with an optional (slightly less stable) performance mode. Co-op works well across both platforms, split-screen is available on PS5, and cross-save between PC and PS5 means switching setups mid-playthrough isn't a hassle.

Our Take

Baldur's Gate 3 raises the bar for what a big-budget RPG can let you get away with before the systems buckle. Act 3 keeps it from being perfect, but nothing else out right now matches this combination of tactical combat, writing, and genuine consequence for what you do.

Pros

  • Genuinely reacts to what you actually do, not just dialogue picks
  • 5E combat rewards using the environment, not just stat-checking
  • Companion writing and voice work is a cut above
  • Co-op, split-screen, and cross-save all just work

Cons

  • Act 3 is a noticeable step down in polish from Acts 1 and 2
  • Frame dips in the city late-game, even on decent PCs
  • Inventory turns into a mess without third-party mods
  • Some quests need real exploring to even trigger
