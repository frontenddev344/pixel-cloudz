The Review

We went in expecting Dark Souls with a bigger map. That's not really what Elden Ring is. FromSoftware kept the combat that made them famous — slow, deliberate, punishing if you get greedy — but instead of funneling you down a single corridor, they just... let you go. Ride off in whatever direction looks interesting and you'll usually find something worth the detour: a buried dungeon, a boss nobody warned you about, a whole side area that isn't on any map until you stumble into it.

The Open World Thing Actually Works Here

Open-world fatigue is real, and we said as much going in. But Elden Ring gets away with it because almost nothing in the Lands Between feels like filler. Legacy dungeons hit as hard as anything in the older Souls games. Optional bosses show up with more production value than some studios put into a final boss. There's a real skill to how the game points you toward things without ever slapping a quest marker on your screen — you just see something glowing on a distant cliff and go "okay, fine, I'll go look." Torrent (the horse, if you haven't played it yet) makes the traveling part painless without making the world feel small.

Combat: More Options, Same Bite

The weapon arts, ashes of war, spirit summons and sorceries give you way more room to build a character than any prior FromSoftware game — if pure strength builds bored you before, there's a whole caster or summon-focused playstyle waiting here. None of it makes the game easy, to be clear. Boss fights are still the highlight of the whole experience, though a few of the later ones (we're looking at you, Malenia) lean so hard into rapid combo strings that they start to feel like they were designed for a 1v1 arena game, not an open world RPG where you might be under-leveled and alone.

Story You Have To Go Digging For

If you want a clean, cutscene-driven plot, this isn't your game — George R. R. Martin's fingerprints are on the lore and mythology, not on any kind of clear narration. You piece the story together from item descriptions and half-cryptic NPCs, which is either infuriating or the whole appeal depending on who you ask. What we will say without reservation: the sound design is doing a lot of heavy lifting here. Wind, distant monster growls, the way music kicks in right as a boss's health bar shows up — it all sells the scale of the place even when nothing is technically happening on screen.

Performance Has Gotten Better, Mostly

Launch-day Elden Ring stuttered a lot, especially on PC and on base PS4/Xbox One. Several patches later, and especially since the Shadow of the Erdtree update, it runs a lot more consistently on current-gen consoles and anything reasonably modern on PC. If you're still on base last-gen hardware though, expect the frame rate to dip whenever things get busy on screen.

The Bottom Line

Elden Ring is big, but it doesn't coast on being big — almost everything you find feels like someone sat down and designed it on purpose. It demands patience and a tolerance for not being told what's going on, and it won't click for everyone. For the people willing to put in the time, though, this is one of the best action RPGs we've played in years.

Pros
  • Boss fights are consistently the best part of playing this
  • Almost every corner of the map has something worth finding
  • Builds are way more flexible than in past Souls games
  • Sound design carries a lot of the atmosphere
Cons
  • You'll get zero hand-holding on the story
  • A few late bosses feel built for arena 1v1s, not open-world play
  • Base PS4/Xbox One still stutters here and there
  • Inventory menus haven't really changed since Dark Souls 1
